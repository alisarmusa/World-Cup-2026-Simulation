    Tournament Overview

    Executive Summary

    The 2026 World Cup is under way, having opened on June 11 in Mexico City, and for the first time in history, 48 nations are competing across three countries — the United States, Canada, and Mexico. The expanded format creates a round-of-32 buffer, simultaneously reducing the risk for elite sides and offering more nations a genuine path to the knockout stages. The net effect: the traditional powerhouses carry even more structural advantages than usual, while a meaningful wave of outsiders can realistically dream of quarterfinal football.

    The central storyline of this tournament is European hegemony at its most concentrated — and, in the Round of 32, its fragility. France have surged to the front of the model and cruised into the last 16, with Spain, England and Portugal close behind. Yet the same opening knockout round that confirmed the favourites also claimed two European heavyweights: Germany and the Netherlands both went out on penalties on the same night. The elite tier remains overwhelmingly European, a reflection of tactical density and infrastructure that has widened the gap at the top — but the expanded bracket has shown it guarantees nothing.

    And yet: there are genuine disruptors, and several have carried their form into the knockouts. Norway returned from a 28-year absence with arguably the most dangerous player in the tournament, and Erling Haaland’s late winner past Ivory Coast took them into the last 16 for the first time in the modern era. Morocco, the 2022 semi-finalists, ground down the Netherlands and won the shootout to reach the last 16, while co-hosts Canada and Mexico both made history with knockout wins of their own. Colombia — one of the most complete rosters in the field and Group K winners — are still to come, backed by one of the loudest fanbases in North America. Japan, by contrast, embodied the round’s cruelty: their best-ever squad pushed Brazil to the 96th minute before bowing out.

    The most significant structural tension: Can defending champions Argentina — aging in key positions, dependent on a 38-year-old Messi — hold together long enough to make a deep run? Messi has answered emphatically so far: a hat-trick against Algeria and then a brace to beat Austria 2–0 that took him past Klose as the World Cup’s all-time leading scorer and sealed top spot in Group J. The deeper concern about whether the squad has evolved beyond him remains. The talent is undiminished; the durability across seven games is the open question.

    The group stage is complete, and the co-hosts set the pace: Mexico won Group A with a perfect three from three, while the United States took Group D despite a dead-rubber 3–2 loss to Türkiye, with Australia advancing second. Switzerland, Brazil, Germany, the Netherlands, France, Spain and Belgium claimed the other groups, before the final round on June 27 settled the last three: England topped Group L as Harry Kane became their all-time World Cup top scorer in a 2–0 win over Panama; Argentina won Group J with Messi off the bench in a 3–1 defeat of Jordan; and Colombia held Portugal to a goalless draw to win Group K. The night’s romance belonged to Cape Verde, the smallest nation ever to reach a World Cup knockout round, and to DR Congo, who came from behind to beat Uzbekistan 3–1 and sneak through. With the highest-scoring group stage on record now in the books, all 32 places were set, and the knockouts are well under way: in the brand-new Round of 32 opener, Canada needed a Stephen Eustáquio strike deep in stoppage time to edge South Africa 1–0 in Los Angeles and reach the last 16 for the first time in their history. Monday then delivered drama and upsets: Brazil survived a Japan scare to win 2–1 through Casemiro and a Gabriel Martinelli stoppage-time winner, but two European giants fell on penalties — Paraguay knocked out Germany 4–3 after a 1–1 draw, and Morocco eliminated the Netherlands 3–2 following Issa Diop’s late leveller, setting up a Canada–Morocco last-16 tie. Tuesday’s three heavyweight ties then went to form up top: France dismantled Sweden 3–0 behind a Mbappé brace, Norway edged Ivory Coast 2–1 through a late Haaland winner to reach the last 16 for the first time in the modern era, and co-hosts Mexico beat Ecuador 2–0 at a roaring Azteca. Three first-round ties remain to complete the bracket — England v DR Congo, Belgium v Senegal and USA v Bosnia — but with Germany and the Netherlands already gone, half the draw has blown wide open.
